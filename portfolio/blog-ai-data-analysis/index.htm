How AI Is Changing Data Analysis Workflows

Artificial intelligence is rapidly becoming part of everyday data and commercial analysis work. While much of the discussion online focuses on automation and productivity gains, the reality inside analytical teams is more nuanced.

For many analysts, AI is changing not only how work gets completed, but also how processes are designed, reviewed and improved. Tasks that once felt efficient enough are now being re-evaluated through the lens of automation, scalability and workflow optimisation.

One of the biggest changes is the expectation around efficiency. Previously, building reports, updating financial models, maintaining dashboards and producing stakeholder commentary followed familiar processes. Analysts knew the logic, understood the workflows and could confidently deliver outputs using established methods.

AI changes that dynamic completely.

There is now an additional layer of thinking involved in almost every task. Once a report or process is complete, the next question becomes whether parts of it could be streamlined further using AI tools, automation or workflow integrations. That creates a constant cycle of experimentation and learning, particularly because the capabilities of AI are still evolving at such a rapid pace.

Reducing manual data handling

In practice, one of the most valuable applications of AI has been reducing manual data handling. Many teams still spend significant amounts of time exporting reports, downloading CSV files, copying data between systems and refreshing working models manually.

Using tools such as Power Query alongside AI-generated code and guidance can dramatically simplify these workflows. Processes that previously required multiple manual steps can often be converted into direct system connections with automated refresh functionality. The technology itself is not necessarily new, but AI lowers the technical barrier to implementing it.

This is particularly useful for recurring analytical processes such as:

  • Financial and operational reporting
  • Dashboard refreshes
  • Customer and sales data extraction
  • Monthly reporting packs
  • Variance analysis preparation
  • KPI tracking and performance monitoring

Performance analysis and stakeholder reporting

Another area where AI is proving useful is performance analysis and stakeholder reporting. Reviewing large datasets often involves analysing multiple dimensions simultaneously including products, regions, channels, customers, operational metrics and financial performance.

AI can help generate first draft commentary by reviewing structured datasets and identifying key movements, anomalies and trends. The most effective approach is not to allow AI to build the analysis entirely from scratch. Strong results usually come from providing a clearly structured template with predefined logic, helper columns and guidance on what the analysis should focus on.

This is where technical and commercial skills still matter heavily. Experienced analysts already have established ways of interpreting data and presenting insights to stakeholders. AI works far better when it is guided within those frameworks rather than being left to determine the structure independently.

A useful comparison is working with a junior analyst. Without context or examples, outputs can vary significantly from expectations. With proper templates, examples and guidance, the quality improves substantially.

Structuring data for AI

Structuring data specifically for AI has also become increasingly important. Clear column naming, helper fields and explicit instructions within datasets can improve output quality considerably.

Simple adjustments can make a major difference:

  • Separate current year, budget and prior year columns
  • Include clear include/exclude flags
  • Add qualitative operational commentary alongside quantitative data
  • Standardise field naming conventions
  • Consolidate supporting information into one working file

This becomes especially valuable when analysing commercial performance, customer behaviour or operational trends where context matters just as much as the raw numbers themselves.

Accelerating ad hoc analysis

AI is also helping accelerate ad hoc analysis. Analysts regularly receive unfamiliar datasets from different departments and historically would spend time manually identifying relationships, operational drivers and useful fields for investigation.

AI can now assist with early-stage exploratory analysis by identifying patterns, highlighting potential relationships and suggesting areas worth investigating further. In many cases, it can complete a large portion of the initial groundwork before human review begins.

Validation remains essential. AI can produce polished commentary and seemingly accurate analysis while still missing important business context or making incorrect assumptions.

Formula checking, data validation and workbook reviews are also not fully reliable without manual verification. The current reality is that AI works best as an accelerator rather than a replacement for analytical thinking.

The growing productivity gap

The analysts gaining the most value from AI are usually those with strong existing foundations in:

  • Data analysis
  • Commercial understanding
  • Data modelling and structuring
  • Process design
  • Reporting logic
  • Stakeholder communication

One of the biggest long-term implications of AI in analytical roles is the growing productivity gap between professionals who actively integrate AI into their workflows and those who do not.

Analysts using AI effectively are already able to complete reporting, commentary, exploratory analysis and data preparation significantly faster than traditional manual workflows allow. Tasks that previously required several hours can now often be completed in a fraction of the time with the right combination of automation, structured data and AI-assisted analysis.

The advantage is not simply about working faster. It is about having more time available for deeper analysis, stakeholder engagement, commercial thinking and decision support.

The strongest analysts will still need technical skills, business understanding and analytical judgement. AI does not remove the need for those capabilities. In many cases, it increases their importance because human review, validation and interpretation remain critical.

As AI tools continue to improve, analysts who understand how to structure workflows around them will likely operate at a very different level of efficiency compared to those relying entirely on traditional manual processes.

For data, finance and commercial analysts, the opportunity is significant. The challenge now is learning how to integrate AI into existing workflows in a way that genuinely improves efficiency without sacrificing quality, governance or critical thinking.
