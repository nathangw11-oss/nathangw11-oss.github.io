Digital Transformation Business Case Model

A fully dynamic investment appraisal model built for a fictional government digital transformation initiative, featuring scenario analysis, NPV and IRR, sensitivity testing, and an executive-ready one-page output, structured the way it would be built on the job.

The Brief

Most financial models sit at one of two extremes: either too simple to credibly support a major investment decision, or technically thorough but impenetrable in a boardroom. The goal here was to build something that sits squarely in the middle, structurally rigorous, grounded in real financial concepts, and clear enough for a non-technical executive to walk away understanding the recommendation.

The scenario is a digital transformation initiative within a government department, a realistic and common setting for commercial advisory work. The model appraises the investment over a 7-year horizon, comparing implementation costs and ongoing operating costs against quantified efficiency benefits, and produces a clear go/no-go recommendation backed by NPV, IRR, and scenario analysis.

The Approach

The model is built on a single controlling principle: every number flowing into the outputs traces back to one place. A dedicated Assumptions tab holds all inputs, covering implementation costs, ongoing costs, benefit estimates, ramp-up curves, growth rates, and the discount rate. Changing the scenario dropdown from Base Case to Conservative or Optimistic updates every figure across every tab at once, without touching a single formula.

Implementation costs are front-loaded in Year 1 with a contingency buffer applied as a percentage of the base costs. Ongoing operating costs escalate year-on-year using an explicit cost escalation rate. Benefits are modelled with a ramp-up curve, with partial realisation in Years 1 and 2 to reflect real-world implementation lag, before reaching full run-rate and growing modestly thereafter. Assuming full benefits from day one would inflate the NPV and undermine the credibility of the analysis, so the ramp-up is a deliberate modelling choice rather than a conservative gesture.

The discount rate is scenario-specific rather than hardcoded, because the cost of capital is itself an assumption that shifts across government contexts and risk profiles. Net cash flows are discounted year by year, and both NPV and IRR are calculated dynamically off those discounted flows.

Colour convention: Blue text is a hardcoded input, black is a formula, and green is a cross-sheet link. Any finance professional will immediately know which cells are assumptions and which are derived.

Key Features

Single-cell scenario switching. Changing one dropdown on the Assumptions tab cascades through every formula across the Cost-Benefit, Scenario Analysis, and Exec Dashboard tabs instantly, avoiding manual overrides and hidden hardcodes.

Benefit ramp-up modelling. Benefits don't reach full run-rate on day one. Year 1 and Year 2 ramp-up percentages are applied before realisation is treated as complete, giving a more credible assumption that holds up to scrutiny in an executive setting.

Two-variable sensitivity table. The Scenario Analysis tab includes a data table showing NPV across a matrix of discount rates and benefit realisation percentages, the two assumptions that most directly determine whether the investment creates value.

Tornado chart data pre-built. Six key assumptions are stress-tested at plus and minus 10% with NPV impact calculated for each, ranked by swing size. The data is structured and ready to drop into a bar chart for the executive pack.

Audit and integrity tab. Seven automated checks flag Pass or Fail, including BCR above 1.0, discount rate within valid bounds, and scenario cell validation. A habit borrowed from professional modelling practice that signals structural confidence.

Executive Dashboard built for the room. KPI cards, an annual cash flow summary, and a pre-written recommendation narrative sit on a single tab, formatted to print as a one-pager and structured to be walked through in an executive meeting without opening another tab.

What I'd Do Differently

In a real engagement, three things would change. The benefit assumptions would be built collaboratively with the business units responsible for delivering the change, with FTE savings in particular needing sign-off from operations rather than being modelled top-down. A Monte Carlo simulation layer would sit alongside the static sensitivity table, giving a probability distribution of outcomes rather than a point-in-time grid. And the model would connect to a live data source for actuals tracking after approval, so the business case develops from a one-off appraisal tool into an ongoing performance management framework. The architecture already supports this approach; what a portfolio model cannot replicate is the stakeholder input and real data that sit behind it.

Business Impact

Under the Base Case, the model returns a positive NPV of approximately $2.3M over 7 years, an IRR of 18.4% against a 7.0% discount rate, and a payback period of under 4 years, pointing clearly to proceeding with the investment. The investment also holds up under the Conservative scenario provided benefit realisation stays above 80%, which the sensitivity table makes immediately visible without any additional calculation.

The real value of the model is not in the output figures themselves but in the questions it surfaces. How confident are we in the benefit ramp-up timeline? What happens to the business case if the discount rate rises? Is the contingency buffer adequate given the project complexity? A well-structured model brings those conversations forward before they become problems, which is the core purpose of commercial advisory work.

Built the way it would be built on the job: assumptions separated from outputs, scenario-ready, and structured for an executive audience. Changing one cell in the Assumptions tab updates every figure across the model.
